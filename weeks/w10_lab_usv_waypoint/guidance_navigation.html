Guidance & Navigation — How the Waypoint Mission Works

Companion to Lab 3

← Back to Lab 3 overview

Why this page exists

The stabilization layer you tuned in Lab 2 knows nothing about waypoints. It chases a speed setpoint and a yaw-rate setpoint, and that is all. For the waypoint mission, something has to produce those setpoints — turning a list of (lat, lon) waypoints into a sequence of “right now, the boat should be moving at 1.4 m/s and turning at 0.3 rad/s.”

That “something” is the guidance and navigation (G&N) stack. This page explains what it is, framed in terms students have already seen: a feedforward + PID cascade, just at a higher level of abstraction.

A familiar pattern: feedforward + PID, recursively

In Lab 2 each stabilization channel had the same internal structure:

setpoint ──► (+)───► PID ───► (+)───► actuator command ──► plant ──► measurement
              ▲              ▲                              │
              │              │                              │
              │              └──── FF (= setpoint × K_dc⁻¹) │
              │                                             │
              └─────────────────── measurement ◄────────────┘

The feedforward branch makes an open-loop guess of the actuator command that should produce the desired output if the plant matched its nominal model. The PID branch closes the loop and corrects whatever the FF guess got wrong.

ArduRover’s G&N stack applies the same pattern one level up. Its “feedforward” is a kinematically-feasible reference trajectory (computed in advance from the waypoints). Its “PID” closes the loop on position error. And the output of that outer loop becomes the setpoint for the inner loop you tuned in Lab 2.

waypoints ──► trajectory gen ──► (pos_ref, vel_ref) ──► (+)──► PID ──► speed_setpoint   ┐
   (open loop, kinematic)                ▲                       └──► yaw_setpoint    ──┘──►  STABILIZATION
                                         │                                                    (Lab 2)
                                  pos_measured ◄── EKF ◄── GPS + IMU

Two layers of “FF + PID,” nested. The outer FF is vel_ref from the S-curve generator. The outer PID is AC_PosControl. The inner FF + PID is what you already have working.

The cascade in three layers

Layer What it does Inputs Outputs
Trajectory generation (S-curve, in AC_WPNav) Plans a smooth path between waypoints that respects vehicle acceleration / jerk / speed limits. Open-loop — no feedback. Waypoint list; WP_SPEED, WP_RADIUS, ATC_ACCEL_MAX, ATC_DECEL_MAX, ATC_TURN_MAX_G, jerk limits Time series of (target position, target velocity, target acceleration) along the path
Position control (AC_PosControl) Closes the outer loop on position error. Same PID + FF structure as Lab 2, just on a slower, geometric quantity. Target pos/vel from trajectory gen; measured position from EKF Speed setpoint (throttle channel) and yaw-rate setpoint (steering channel)
Stabilization (Lab 2) Closes the inner loop on speed and yaw-rate errors. Two independent PID + FF loops. Setpoints from AC_PosControl; measured speed (GPS_Spd/EKF), measured yaw rate (gyro/EKF) Throttle PWM (RCOU_C3), rudder PWM (RCOU_C1)

The handoff between layers 2 and 3 happens at the exact same channels you logged in Lab 2:

  • Position controller’s speed output → THR_DesSpeed → speed PID input
  • Position controller’s yaw-rate output → STER_DesTurnRate → yaw-rate PID input

That is the bridge from Lab 2 to Lab 3. In Lab 2 you saw clean step responses on these channels because the pilot generated the steps. In Lab 3 you will see a more complex, time-varying reference on the same channels — generated by AC_PosControl chasing the S-curve.

The S-curve, briefly

ArduPilot’s S-curve is not a Bézier curve. It is a jerk-limited 7-segment kinematic trajectory:

“SCurves calculate paths between waypoints (including the corners) using specified speed, acceleration and jerk limits.” — libraries/AP_Math/SCurve.h

The 7 segments are: jerk-up → constant accel → jerk-down → cruise → jerk-down (decel) → constant decel → jerk-up. The corner-blending between consecutive waypoints uses a spline so the path stays continuous in velocity, but the speed profile along that path is the jerk-limited segmented profile, not a Bézier in time.

The point of all this: the S-curve produces a reference that the boat is physically capable of following — it will not ask for an acceleration the actuators cannot deliver, or a turn rate the hull cannot sustain at the requested speed. From the Rover docs:

“S-Curves are used to plan a smooth path that brings the vehicle close to each waypoint while not exceeding speed or acceleration limits. […] The vehicle will cut the corners more at higher speeds and more if the maximum accelerations are reduced, but will slow down in the corners if necessary to pass within WP_RADIUS of the waypoint without exceeding the maximum accelerations.”

This is feedforward in the strictest sense: an open-loop plan generated from the waypoints and the vehicle’s kinematic limits, fed into the loop below as a reference.

Closing the position loop (AC_PosControl)

Layer 2 — the position controller — looks like a standard PID with feedforward, exactly as you saw in Lab 2:

Term Parameter Role
FF (velocity) (from S-curve) open-loop velocity reference along the planned path
P (position) PSC_POS_P proportional gain on position error
P, I, D (velocity) PSC_VEL_P, PSC_VEL_I, PSC_VEL_D inner velocity loop inside the position controller

Position error feeds a P controller that produces a desired velocity correction; that gets added to the S-curve’s vel_ref to produce the composite velocity setpoint; the velocity PID then produces a throttle/rudder demand — which, for Rover, is delivered as a speed setpoint to your Lab-2 PIDs rather than directly to the motors.

If that structure feels recursive, that is exactly the point. It is the same Lab-2 block diagram with one extra outer wrap.

Why Lab 2 matters

Here is the punchline for the course: the G&N stack only generates setpoints that are achievable in the ideal. Whether the boat actually achieves them is the inner loop’s job.

If your stabilization PIDs from Lab 2 are sluggish, the boat will lag the S-curve and overshoot corners. If they are oscillatory, the trajectory will be tracked with high-frequency jitter on top. If they saturate, the S-curve’s “kinematic feasibility” guarantee is broken — the plant cannot deliver what was planned.

A well-tuned trajectory on top of poorly-tuned PIDs is wasted. A well-tuned stabilization layer is what enables the outer loops to do their job. That is why we spent a whole lab on it.

What you tune (and what you don’t)

For fairness across teams, most G&N parameters are fixed in the baseline .param file. The handles that do affect mission performance and are worth understanding:

Parameter Effect
WP_SPEED Target cruise speed along the path (m/s)
WP_RADIUS How close the boat has to get to a waypoint to count as arrived (m)
WP_OVERSHOOT Maximum allowed lateral overshoot when cutting corners (m)
ATC_ACCEL_MAX, ATC_DECEL_MAX Speed-axis acceleration caps used by the S-curve generator
ATC_TURN_MAX_G Lateral-acceleration cap used by the S-curve generator

PSC_* (position-controller PID gains) are not part of Lab 3 tuning. They live in the baseline .param and stay fixed across teams.
